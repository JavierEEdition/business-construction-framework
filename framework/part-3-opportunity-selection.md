# Part III — Opportunity Selection

## Purpose

The purpose of this part is to select the economic terrain in which the builder should accumulate knowledge, capabilities, relationships and ownership over the long term.

The objective is not merely to identify an interesting business idea. It is to compare the relevant business universe systematically and select a vertical capable of supporting a long process of value creation, business construction and capital accumulation.

The final result of Part III must be:

> A selected, justified and prioritised vertical.

At the end of this part, the builder should be able to say:

> I will dedicate the next decade to learning and creating value in this terrain.

This part follows six stages:

1. Business world taxonomy.
2. Industry universe.
3. First screening.
4. Deep research.
5. Comparison.
6. Vertical selection.

Each section must produce a real asset, decision or document. Understanding alone is not considered a sufficient outcome.

# Chapter 1 — Business World Taxonomy

## 1.1 Taxonomy

Before deciding which business to build, it is necessary to understand what kinds of businesses exist.

The purpose of the taxonomy is not to evaluate, filter or select opportunities. Its purpose is to create a complete conceptual map of the business world and prevent the search from being limited to the first ideas that come to mind.

A business opportunity can be analysed through several distinct dimensions:

### Economic terrain

The industry or vertical in which the company operates.

Examples include industrial maintenance, dental laboratories, freight forwarding, water treatment and vertical software.

### Customer type

The dominant customer served by the business.

The principal categories are:

* B2B: businesses serving other businesses.
* B2C: businesses serving consumers.
* B2G: businesses serving governments or public institutions.
* Mixed models serving more than one category.

### Value-creation mechanism

The principal way in which the business creates value.

Examples include:

* manufacturing;
* distribution;
* technical or professional services;
* recurring operations;
* software and data;
* financial intermediation;
* ownership and exploitation of assets;
* platforms and marketplaces;
* information and intellectual property.

### Revenue model

The principal mechanism through which value is captured.

Examples include:

* recurring contracts;
* subscriptions;
* individual transactions;
* projects;
* commissions;
* licensing;
* usage-based revenue;
* asset income;
* combinations of several mechanisms.

### Position in the value chain

The role occupied by the business within the wider economic system.

Examples include:

* raw material provider;
* manufacturer;
* distributor;
* installer;
* operator;
* maintainer;
* inspector;
* software provider;
* intermediary;
* owner of the underlying asset.

### Business stage and vehicle

The same economic terrain can be entered through different vehicles.

Examples include:

* consulting;
* productised services;
* managed services;
* software;
* SaaS;
* distribution;
* acquisition of an existing company;
* minority ownership;
* buy-and-build.

These vehicles must not be confused with industries. Software, consulting and acquisition are ways of entering or operating within a terrain, not terrains in themselves.

### Geography and institutions

The economics of a business are also affected by:

* regulation;
* labour markets;
* taxation;
* access to capital;
* culture;
* infrastructure;
* market maturity;
* institutional stability.

Geography is therefore an important analytical layer, but it should not initially replace the selection of the economic terrain.

### Time and technological change

The attractiveness of a terrain can change because of:

* technological shifts;
* demographic changes;
* regulation;
* labour shortages;
* consolidation;
* climate transition;
* changes in customer expectations;
* new methods of organising work.

Timing must be analysed separately from the underlying structural quality of the industry.

## Asset produced

A multidimensional map of the business world separating:

* economic terrain;
* customer;
* value-creation mechanism;
* revenue model;
* value-chain position;
* business vehicle;
* geography;
* timing.

This map prevents the Framework from confusing an industry, a business model, a product, a country and a company.

## 1.2 How to Traverse the Taxonomy

A taxonomy is only useful if it can be explored systematically.

The exploration process follows a top-down sequence:

1. Divide the economy into broad macrosectors.
2. Decompose every macrosector into industries.
3. Decompose each industry into sufficiently homogeneous verticals.
4. Classify each vertical by customer, economic model and revenue model.
5. Consolidate overlaps.
6. Preserve economically distinct categories.
7. Submit the resulting universe to the Business Construction Matrix.

The process must follow several rules.

### Start broadly

The search must include:

* traditional and emerging sectors;
* physical and digital businesses;
* B2B, B2C and B2G;
* services, manufacturing, distribution, software and asset-based businesses;
* mature and growing industries;
* businesses entered organically and businesses entered through acquisition.

The purpose is to avoid beginning with a preferred answer.

### Separate terrain from vehicle

A vertical describes the system in which knowledge accumulates.

A vehicle describes how the builder initially enters or captures value within that system.

For example:

* logistics is a terrain;
* consulting, managed services and software are possible vehicles within logistics.

### Use economically meaningful units

A category is useful only when its companies share enough of the following:

* customers;
* activities;
* economics;
* regulation;
* operational problems;
* suppliers;
* performance metrics;
* transferable knowledge.

A category that is too broad hides important economic differences.

A category that is too narrow creates unnecessary fragmentation.

### Search for omissions

Before screening begins, the universe must be challenged deliberately.

The process should ask:

* Which large economic activities are missing?
* Which unattractive or difficult industries have been omitted because of bias?
* Which categories are merely vehicles rather than terrains?
* Which categories combine businesses with incompatible economics?
* Which categories are duplicates under different names?

### Stop expanding before evaluation

Once the universe is reasonably comprehensive and the categories are sufficiently homogeneous, exploration must stop.

New candidates should only be added later when they represent a materially different economic system. Minor variants should be analysed within an existing vertical.

## Asset produced

A repeatable procedure for exploring the business universe without relying on memory, fashion or initial preferences.

# Chapter 2 — Industry Universe

## 2.1 Construction of the Universe

The first level of the opportunity universe divides the economy into broad families.

The Business Construction Framework uses 18 economic families:

1. Agriculture, resources and food.
2. Manufacturing and materials.
3. Pharmaceuticals, biotechnology and medical devices.
4. Construction and specialty trades.
5. Field services and facility services.
6. Energy, water, waste and environment.
7. Specialised B2B distribution.
8. Transport and logistics.
9. Healthcare and care services.
10. Professional and business services.
11. Inspection, testing and certification.
12. Real estate and property management.
13. Education and training.
14. Finance and insurance.
15. Retail, consumer, hospitality and personal services.
16. Software, data and digital infrastructure.
17. Outsourced public services and infrastructure.
18. Emerging technologies and business models.

These families are not evaluation units. They are navigation devices used to guarantee broad coverage.

A macrosector is normally too heterogeneous to receive a meaningful Business Construction Matrix assessment. Its purpose is to ensure that no major area of economic activity disappears from the search.

## Asset produced

An inventory of 18 macrosectors covering the principal forms of economic activity relevant to business construction.

## 2.2 Decomposition into Industries

Each macrosector was decomposed into industries with more coherent economic characteristics.

This stage produced approximately 140–170 industries, depending on the precise level of granularity used.

The inventory included, among many others:

* extensive agriculture;
* food processing;
* precision manufacturing;
* industrial machinery;
* pharmaceutical manufacturing;
* clinical research;
* commercial construction;
* HVAC;
* industrial maintenance;
* water treatment;
* specialised distribution;
* freight forwarding;
* healthcare clinics;
* care services;
* accounting and payroll;
* testing and certification;
* property management;
* vocational training;
* insurance brokerage;
* hospitality;
* vertical software;
* managed IT;
* outsourced public services;
* emerging industrial technologies.

The purpose was breadth rather than final precision.

At this level, important differences remained hidden. A hospital does not share the same economics as a dental clinic. A telecommunications operator does not share the same economics as a managed network provider. A general construction company does not share the same economics as a fire-protection maintenance company.

The industry inventory therefore served as an intermediate layer between macrosectors and vertical candidates.

## Asset produced

A broad inventory of approximately 140–170 industries providing sufficient coverage for further decomposition.

---

## 2.3 Decomposition into Verticals

The next stage converted industries into units that could eventually be compared and selected.

A vertical candidate is defined as:

> A sufficiently homogeneous group of companies sharing customers, activities, economics, regulation and operational problems, so that knowledge acquired in one company can be reused in others.

This definition is stricter than a conventional industry label.

The decomposition initially produced approximately 450 raw vertical candidates across 25 exploratory families.

The raw inventory was deliberately expansive. It included:

* large and small markets;
* attractive and unattractive industries;
* traditional and emerging activities;
* capital-light and capital-intensive models;
* regulated and unregulated sectors;
* physical and digital businesses;
* fragmented and concentrated industries.

The purpose was not to claim that all 450 candidates deserved individual analysis. The purpose was to expose the entire search space before consolidation.

Each candidate was examined using dimensions such as:

* dominant customer;
* principal activity;
* economic model;
* revenue model;
* regulation;
* operational similarity;
* transferability of knowledge;
* overlap with other candidates.

The raw list was then consolidated to eliminate:

* duplicate categories;
* categories separated only by minor product differences;
* vehicles incorrectly treated as industries;
* categories too broad for meaningful comparison;
* categories too narrow to justify separate strategic treatment.

## Asset produced

A broad raw inventory of approximately 450 vertical candidates and a repeatable definition for deciding what constitutes a strategically meaningful vertical.

The raw inventory is preserved in the historical BCF working document. It is not reproduced in this Markdown file because the operative universe is maintained separately in the master catalogue.

---

## 2.4 Consolidated Opportunity Universe

The consolidation stage transformed the raw universe into the official set of candidates used by the first screening.

The official catalogue contains:

* 18 economic families;
* 184 coded vertical candidates;
* traditional, technological, regulated, physical and digital businesses;
* B2B, B2C and B2G models;
* deliberately attractive and deliberately difficult candidates;
* sufficiently homogeneous units for initial evaluation.

The catalogue is stored in:

`master-industry-catalog.csv`

Each candidate contains:

* a unique code;
* its economic family;
* a vertical name;
* a short neutral description;
* dominant customer type;
* dominant business model;
* dominant revenue model;
* current screening status;
* decision fields;
* evidence and notes fields.

The catalogue is the operational source of truth for the screening process.

The narrative Framework explains the method and conclusions. The CSV contains the complete candidate-level data.

### Rules of the consolidated universe

All 184 candidates enter the first screening.

Candidates will not be removed without an explicit reason.

Uncertainty alone is not grounds for elimination.

When the available evidence is insufficient, the candidate survives until a later stage.

New candidates should only be added when they represent a materially different economic system that is not already covered.

Minor variations should be investigated inside the relevant existing vertical.

## Asset produced

BCF Opportunity Universe v1.0:

* 184 coded and delimited vertical candidates;
* organised across 18 economic families;
* ready to receive the first economic screening;
* maintained in `master-industry-catalog.csv`.

The next section, 3.1 Economic Screening, will apply Pillar 1 of the Business Construction Matrix to the complete universe.

It will classify candidates into:

1. Eliminated because of structural economic defects.
2. Survivors with reservations.
3. Clear survivors.

Every elimination must contain an explicit reason.

No candidate will disappear merely because it feels unattractive.
