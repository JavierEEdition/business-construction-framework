# Chapter 3 — First Screening

## 3.1 Economic Screening

The Master Industry Catalogue produced in the previous chapter deliberately contains a very broad set of candidate verticals.

Some are outstanding.

Some are mediocre.

Some are economically unattractive almost regardless of who manages them.

The purpose of the first screening stage is not to identify the best industries.

It is to eliminate the worst ones.

This distinction is fundamental.

A common mistake in entrepreneurship is to compare every opportunity against an abstract ideal of what a perfect business would look like.

The Business Construction Framework does not work that way.

At this stage we ask a much simpler question:

Does this industry possess structural economic characteristics that make long-term value creation unusually difficult?

If the answer is yes, the candidate is removed before further effort is invested.

The objective is therefore asymmetric.

We are not trying to predict extraordinary success.

We are trying to avoid predictable failure.

This screening applies only Pillar 1 of the Business Construction Matrix: Economic Quality of the System.

No consideration is yet given to:

* the personal strengths of the entrepreneur;
* operational excellence;
* technological innovation;
* ownership opportunities;
* acquisition strategies;
* capital allocation.

Those questions belong to later stages.

A mediocre entrepreneur may occasionally build a good company inside a good industry.

An exceptional entrepreneur may sometimes rescue a poor business.

But repeatedly attempting to compensate for structurally weak economics is rarely an efficient allocation of entrepreneurial effort.

For this reason the Framework prefers to remove industries with fundamentally unattractive economics before analysing any other dimension.

### Screening criteria

Each candidate is evaluated against a small number of structural questions.

Examples include:

* Is pricing power structurally weak?
* Is competition largely price-based?
* Does the industry require unusually high capital intensity?
* Are margins persistently compressed?
* Is the business heavily exposed to commodity pricing?
* Does value creation depend primarily on scale rather than capability?
* Are returns systematically vulnerable to factors outside managerial control?

No single negative characteristic is sufficient for elimination.

Many outstanding industries possess one or even several weaknesses.

The Framework eliminates only those industries where multiple structural disadvantages combine into an unfavourable economic system.

The emphasis is therefore on economic structure, not on isolated metrics.

### Treatment of uncertainty

Economic screening is intentionally conservative.

Whenever evidence is ambiguous, candidates are not eliminated.

Instead they remain in the catalogue as Survive with Reservations and progress to later stages.

Removing a genuinely attractive industry too early is a more expensive mistake than temporarily analysing a mediocre one.

This principle deliberately biases the screening toward false positives rather than false negatives.

Later chapters provide additional opportunities for elimination.

An industry removed here never receives those opportunities.

### Results of the first screening

Applying the Economic Quality pillar to the catalogue produced three categories.

### 1. Eliminated

Industries whose structural economics are consistently dominated by commodity pricing, extreme capital intensity, chronically weak pricing power or persistent inability to compound value.

Examples include:

* Commodity crop production.
* General road freight.
* Passenger airlines.
* Automobile manufacturing.
* Grocery retail.
* Generic restaurant operations.

These industries may contain excellent companies.

They may also generate exceptional returns during particular cycles.

Nevertheless, as economic systems they require entrepreneurs to fight persistent structural disadvantages from the very beginning.

The Framework therefore excludes them from further consideration.

### 2. Survive with Reservations

Industries that contain meaningful opportunities but also exhibit structural weaknesses requiring deeper investigation.

Typical concerns include:

* cyclical demand;
* high working-capital requirements;
* regulatory dependence;
* customer concentration;
* significant capital expenditure;
* technological uncertainty.

These industries remain under consideration but are explicitly marked as requiring additional evidence before advancing.

### 3. Clear Survivors

Industries displaying structurally attractive economics through combinations of:

* recurring demand;
* specialised knowledge;
* technical differentiation;
* regulation;
* switching costs;
* operational complexity;
* fragmented markets;
* opportunities for long-term capability accumulation.

These candidates advance to the next stage without economic reservations.

The distinction is important.

A Clear Survivor is not necessarily an outstanding business opportunity.

It is simply an industry that has successfully passed the first filter.

Further strategic, operational and ownership analysis may still eliminate it.

### References and intellectual foundations

The economic reasoning behind this screening draws from several complementary streams of research rather than a single source.

The most influential include:

* Michael Porter, Competitive Strategy (1980) and Competitive Advantage (1985), for industry structure and competitive forces.
* Bruce Greenwald & Judd Kahn, Competition Demystified (2005), for structural barriers and competitive advantage.
* Hamilton Helmer, 7 Powers (2016), for durable economic power.
* Richard Rumelt, Good Strategy/Bad Strategy (2011), for strategic diagnosis.
* Warren Buffett's shareholder letters, particularly his distinction between good businesses and good management.
* Charlie Munger's writings and speeches on economic moats and long-term capital allocation.

The Framework does not reproduce any of these models directly.

Instead, it synthesises their common principles into a practical elimination process designed for entrepreneurs selecting a vertical rather than investors selecting a company.

### Deliverable

Economic Screening v1

The Business Construction Framework has now completed its first elimination stage.

Every candidate in the Master Industry Catalogue has been assigned one of three statuses:

* Eliminated
* Survive with Reservations
* Clear Survivor

Each elimination includes an explicit economic justification.

No industry has been removed solely because it appears unattractive, unfamiliar or unfashionable.

From this point onward, every remaining candidate has demonstrated at least acceptable structural economics and is eligible to proceed to the Strategic Screening stage (Section 3.2).
